\begin{abstract}

A tight connection between generative control policies and sampling-based predictive control (SBPC) has recently gained attention in robotics. Enabled by modern computing architectures, this approach leverages both multi-modal action distributions and stochastic optimal control. Specifically, Model Predictive Path Integral (MPPI) control provides rapid, simulated trajectory distributions as regression targets. Flow Matching (FM) policies effectively regress these expert demonstrations, generating diverse behaviors that imitate optimal control at inference time. However, generative policies suffer from mode collapse when trained on a single reward that couples safety and performance, and they often fail in out-of-the-distribution (OOD) scenarios. To address these issues, we propose the offline Safe Flow Expansion framework built upon MPPI and FM. Our approach (1) decouples the dual objectives in the MPPI demonstration data via a single safety parameter, and (2) enlarges the adaptive optimal policy distribution to cover OOD states, independently satisfying both objectives. During deployment, we evaluate the performance of our adaptive design in safety-critical settings in both 2D and 3D cluttered environments.

% Sampling-based predictive control (SBPC) algorithms, such as model predictive path integral (MPPI), have become a popular method for robotic motion planning applications due to their computational efficiency on modern computing architectures. However, it is difficult to enforce hard safety constraints for such techniques because they average over samples to realize the final set of control inputs. While individual samples may satisfy a given constraint, the sample average can violate the safety requirement. Similarly, diffusion and flow matching algorithms have become a popular way to generate robot motion trajectories. In this paper, we introduce a novel strategy to learn a conditional generative model that allows for online tuning of a safety parameter. As for sampling-based controllers, it is difficult to enforce hard safety-critical constraints in these methods. To address these limitations, this paper proposes a safety-critical framework for SBPCs and generative trajectory generation, based on the framework of discrete-time control barrier functions (DCBFs). Our DCBF-based method ensures safety during both the sampling and averaging phases of SBPCs, efficiently producing a rich dataset of multi-modal, collision-free behaviors. We then train a conditional flow matching model on these data to learn a generative policy that can be modulated at inference time by a single safety parameter. We demonstrate our approach for cluttered environments for a double integrator and a quadrotor simulation. Our results indicate that the proposed MPPI-DCBF framework consistently achieves superior safety guarantees compared to baseline MPPI methods, together with a context-aware conditional flow matching model, paving the way for real-time, safe SBPC and generative robotic control.

\section{Introduction}

Sampling-based predictive control (SBPC) has been gaining traction in the nonlinear optimal control community. Completely general optimal control approaches often suffer from computational complexity associated with solving stochastic Hamilton--Jacobi--Bellman equations backwards in time, commonly referred to as the curse of dimensionality. SBPC is an alternative to gradient-based model predictive control (MPC), where a simple sampling procedure is used in place of a sophisticated nonlinear optimizer \cite{posa2014direct, kurtz2023inverse, aydinoglu2024consensus}. Algorithms in this family include model predictive path integral control (MPPI) \cite{williams2016aggressive}, predictive sampling \cite{howell2022predictive}, and cross-entropy methods \cite{rubinstein1999cross}. MPPI, for example, circumvents computational complexity by employing forward sampling of stochastic dynamics combined with Monte Carlo approximation techniques \cite{williams2017model}. The optimal control is then computed as the exponentially weighted average of the sampled trajectories, enabling real-time implementation.
SBPC algorithms are particularly well-suited to recent advances in parallel computing architectures and parallel simulation \cite{mjx, Genesis, makoviychuk2021isaac}, enabling them to scale to complex problems like in-hand dexterous manipulation \cite{li2025drop}, legged locomotion \cite{xue2025full}, and more \cite{howell2022predictive}.

Despite these advantages, SBPCs such as MPPI inherently struggle with safety guarantees, such as collision avoidance. Safety constraints are typically incorporated indirectly through penalty terms in the cost function that are inversely proportional to the robot's proximity to obstacles and heavily penalize the collision~\cite{williams2017model, mohamed2023gp}. However, relying solely on these penalties lacks explicit safety guarantees due to the nature of the sampling averaging procedure. 
This limitation motivates our goal to integrate safety-critical constraints explicitly into the SBPC frameworks.
% \note{Need a brief sentence and an reference about safety filters at the beginning of the next paragraph.}
A common method to ensure safety in collision avoidance tasks is to employ a safety filter, which minimally modifies the output of a potentially unsafe nominal controller to guarantee the satisfaction of the constraints \cite{ames2014cbf}. However, a simple safety filter applied \textit{only} to the control output is insufficient to guarantee safety due to the tight coupling between control objectives and long-term safety criteria that are potentially conflicting. Previous works have used control Lyapunov functions (CLF) and control barrier functions (CBF), often with relaxation factors, to solve this issue as an optimization problem, for mediating safety and feasibility \cite{ames2016control, zeng2021enhancing}, or solve the optimization problem in the context of model predictive control (MPC) to take future information into account over a longer horizon \cite{zeng2021safety}. Recent works have encoded this concept through predictive safety filters, which leverage predictive CBFs in layered control architectures \cite{compton2024learning}. However, in cluttered environments, this direct optimization often becomes non-convex and intractable.

This computational burden motivates a shift from online constrained optimization to learning-based control, where complex planning is performed offline to curate datasets of safe, multi-modal behavior. Generative models, such as diffusion and flow matching, can synthesize motion trajectories from diverse, high-dimensional demonstrations \cite{kurtz2025generative, chi2023diffusion}. However, they do not natively provide formal safety guarantees during rollout. Previous work attempts to encode safety by conditioning or regularizing generation \cite{dai2025safefm, carvalho2023mpdiffusion}, but deployments still lack mechanisms to systematically trade off safety and feasibility. This can be critical in real-world tasks where the robot must adjust caution to context. Recent works have incorporated MPC into refinement of generation to guarantee local optimality in its multi-modal behavior that is induced by reward-based guidance \cite{mizuta2025unified}. This allows generative methods to account for safety and feasibility, but it can struggle to generalize to out-of-distribution (OOD) environments, because this reward-tilting can over-optimize the learned distribution \cite{dandapanthula2026we}.

% \note{Consider adding literature here on dataset aggregation, reachability analysis, or conformal prediction applied to generative policies to address OOD environments.}

A fundamental challenge in generative control is addressing this distribution shift while maintaining safety and feasibility. Recent works suggest that efficient self-generation in flow matching can discover these safe behaviors with probabilistic guarantees through an iterative verification and update process \cite{de2026active}. We extend this concept to a conditional flow matching (CFM) model, explicitly capturing the intrinsic trade-off between safety and feasibility through a verification process to generate a conditioned policy. To this end, we introduce the \textbf{Safe Flow Expansion} framework, a novel approach for \textit{distilling safety} from a robust predictive planner into a generative policy.

\vspace{1mm}
\noindent \textbf{Contributions of the paper.} The primary contributions of this paper are threefold:
(i) An easy-to-simulate, safety-critical predictive planner, MPPI-DCBF, which employs a discrete-time control barrier function (DCBF) to filter unsafe rollouts within a sampling-based framework, implicitly modulating the trade-off between safety and feasibility. 
(ii) A Safe Flow Expansion mechanism that learns the distribution of the planner via self-generation and verification, enabling a real-time generative policy. 
(iii) Comprehensive validation of the proposed framework in a 2D cluttered environment, alongside an extension to 3D drone path planning, demonstrating robust generalization and tunable safety.

Our generative policy builds upon generative predictive control (GPC), which learns a vector field to map noise to control actions conditioned on observations and then applies control in a receding horizon fashion~\cite{kurtz2025generative}. To achieve this, we introduce two key components. First, we propose a novel predictive planner, MPPI-DCBF, which uses an affine discrete-time control barrier function (DCBF) to filter unsafe rollouts within a sampling-based planner. This method generates a rich dataset where behaviors are implicitly modulated by a tunable safety parameter, $\gamma$. Second, we extend the GPC framework by training a conditional flow matching model that uses local geometric information, a sequential context vector, and the safety parameter $\gamma$ to learn a policy that explicitly modulates the trade-off between safety and feasibility at inference time, resolving ambiguities not addressed in the original GPC implementation.

\section{Preliminaries}
\subsection{Model Predictive Path Integral control}
In discrete time, MPPI solves the following non-convex optimization problem at time step $k$ and state $\mathbf{x}(0) \in \mathbb{R}^{n_x}$ over the mean control sequence $\{ \mathbf{u}_{k+i\mid k}\}\in\mathbb{R}^{n_u}$ for $i \in  \{0, ..., N-1\}$
\begin{subequations}
\label{eq:sc-mppi}
\begin{align}
\min_{\{\mathbf{u}_{k+i\mid k}\}}
& \mathbb{E}\!\left[
\phi\!\left(\mathbf{x}_{k+N\mid k}\right)
\!+\!\!\sum_{i=0}^{N-1}\Big(q(\mathbf{x}_{k+i\mid k})\!+\!\tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{u}_{k+i\mid k}^\top R\,\mathbf{u}_{k+i\mid k}\Big)
\right], \label{eq:mppi-cost} \tag{1a}\\
\text{s.t.}\quad
& \mathbf{x}_{k+i+1\mid k}=f\!\left(\mathbf{x}_{k+i\mid k},\,\mathbf{u}_{k+i\mid k}+\varepsilon_{k+i}\right), 
\label{eq:mppi-dyn}  \tag{1b}\\
& \mathbf{x}_{k+i\mid k} \in \mathcal{X}, \quad \mathbf{u}_{k+i\mid k} \in \mathcal{U}, \label{eq:mppi-set1} \tag{1c} \\
& \mathbf{x}_{k\mid k}=\mathbf{x}_k, \quad \mathbf{x}_{k+H-1\mid k} \in \mathcal{X}_f,\qquad \label{eq:mppi-set2} \tag{1d}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
Here, $N$ is the $q: \mathbb{R}^{n_x} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ is any running cost, $\phi : \mathbb{R}^{n_x} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ is a terminal cost, $\varepsilon_{k+i}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\,\Sigma)$ is the control disturbance, $\Sigma$ is the control disturbance variance, $R \in \mathbb{R}^{n_u \times n_u}$ is the control cost weight.
For the collision avoidance task, the controller can reason about crashing by setting collision penalties using the signed clearance to the closest obstacle $d_{\min}(\mathbf{x})$ in their running cost function, such as
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
q(\mathbf{x})
&=(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{goal}})^\top Q (\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{goal}})\\
&\quad+\kappa_{\text{soft}}\,e^{-\alpha\,d_{\min}(\mathbf{x})}
+\kappa_{\text{hard}}\,\mathbf{1}\!\bigl[d_{\min}(\mathbf{x})<0\bigr].
\label{eq:mppisoftconstraint}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The MPPI optimal control sequence is a path-integral approximation of reward-weighted average random variations, which is solved by Monte Carlo methods, centered on the optimal policy sequence at the previous time step \cite{williams2017model}. The unexecuted part of the sequence is shifted by one time step and acts as the warm start of the next optimization. 

Let this warm start sequence from the previous time step be denoted by $\bar{\mathbf{u}}_{k+i\mid k}$. For rollout $m=1,\dots,M$ with perturbations $\varepsilon^{(m)}_{k+i}\!\sim\!\mathcal{N}(0,\Sigma)$, MPPI measures cost per rollout via forward sampling of random controls and states that can be expressed as
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
\mathbf{u}^{(m)}_{k+i\mid i}&=\bar{\mathbf{u}}_{k+i\mid i}+\varepsilon^{(m)}_{k+i},\label{eq:mppi_rollout}\\
\mathbf{x}^{(m)}_{k+i+1\mid i+1}&=f\!\bigl(\mathbf{x}^{(m)}_{k+i\mid i},\,\mathbf{u}^{(m)}_{k+i\mid i}\bigr).
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
Then, the cost for each rollout is
\begin{equation}
S^{(m)}\!=\!\phi\bigl(\mathbf{x}^{(m)}_{k+N\mid N}\bigr)
+\sum_{i=0}^{N-1}\Big(q\,\!\bigl(\mathbf{x}^{(m)}_{k+i\mid i}\bigr)+\tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbf{u}^{(m)\top}_{k+i\mid i} R\,\mathbf{u}^{(m)}_{k+i\mid i}\Big).
\label{eq:mppicost}
\end{equation}
Information-theoretic MPPI uses soft-min importance weights \cite{williams2017info}:
\begin{equation}
\omega_m=\exp\Bigl(-\tfrac{1}{\lambda}\,[S^{(m)}-\beta]\Bigr),\qquad
\beta=\min_m S^{(m)}, \label{eq:mppi-weights}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda >0$ is an inverse temperature which controls how heavily the optimal trajectory is weighted, and $\beta$ is the minimum cost, used to improve the numerical stability. Then, for $i \in \{0, ..., N-1\}$, the mean update that gives optimal control sequence is
\begin{equation}
\Delta \mathbf{u}_{k+i\mid k}=
\frac{\sum_{m=1}^{M}\omega_m\,\varepsilon^{(m)}_{k+i}}{\sum_{m=1}^{M}\omega_m},
\quad
\mathbf{u}^*_{k+i\mid k}\leftarrow \bar{\mathbf{u}}_{k+i\mid k}+\Delta \mathbf{u}_{k+i\mid k}. \label{eq:mppi-update}
\end{equation}
Then the current optimal control $\mathbf{u}^*_{k\mid k}$ is applied. 
% & h^{\mathrm{aff}}\!\left(x_{k+i\mid k};\,x_{k\mid k}\right)\ \ge\ (1-\gamma)^i,\quad i=0,\dots,N. \tag{1d}\label{eq:safemppi}
This parallelizable sampling process makes MPPI highly suitable for solving non-convex optimal control problems on modern GPU hardware. However, a key challenge arises in safety-critical applications. When obstacles are present, a cost for avoiding obstacles does not provide formal safety guarantees, as the final control is averaged over many trajectories. 

Several works have focused on incorporating safety into the forward sampling process. Approaches that shape the rollout distribution or impose barrier constraints \cite{covarianceSteeringSMPC2019,shieldmppi2023} bias the sampling toward safety, but often lack a rigorous formal definition. Conversely, methods that act as sampling-based safety filters by greedily selecting safe samples \cite{rabiee2024guaranteed} enforce safety but bypass the averaging process in \eqref{eq:mppi-update}, thereby sacrificing the optimality certificate.

Conditional Flow matching is increasingly used in robotics for trajectory generation and policy generation \cite{kurtz2025generative,mizuta2025unified}. The trajectory generation process learns a mapping from noise to entire collision-free trajectories by end-to-end supervised learning, but it can struggle to generalize to out-of-distribution environments and may require complete retraining \cite{ye2024efficient, dai2025safefm}. 
% In contrast, (conditional) flow matching-based policy generation is more flexible, focuses on learning a mapping to a lower-dimensional control action, conditioned on observations \cite{kurtz2025generative}.

Here, we describe the important concept of conditional flow matching. For collision avoidance, it is essential to generate a trajectory conditioned on geometric observations while benefiting from offline flow matching training and fast computation from SBPC. To achieve this, the generative model predicts actions conditioned on the given observation $\mathbf{o_t}$ and executes for a short horizon \cite{chi2023diffusion}. 

Concretely, at the current discrete time step $k$, the model takes $O$ steps of observations $\mathbf{o}_{k:k+O-1}$, predicts $H$ steps of actions $\mathbf{\hat u}_{k:k+H-1}$, execute $A$ steps of predicted actions $\mathbf{\hat u}_{k:k+A-1}$. Here, $O$ is the observation horizon, $H$ is the planning horizon, $A$ is the execution horizon of the action. Throughout our experiments, we used fixed values of $O = 1, H = 10, A = 1$. 

It is important to note that our framework utilize safety-critical MPPI planner as a pre-trained model and \textit{distill its safety} to a conditional flow matching. Here, $\mathbf{v}_t(\cdot)$ denotes the flow matching vector field that is learned to regress behavior of the planner. We use \(\textbf{z}\in\mathbb{R}^{n_u}\) for a target optimal control policy, \textit{i.e.,} the symbol \(\textbf{u}^*_{k\mid k}\) used in the previous section.

Conditional Flow matching aims to learn a marginal vector field $\textbf{v}^*_t(x)$ that converts a simple distribution $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_d)$ into a complex data distribution $z \sim p_{data}$. This is done by considering the marginal probability path $p_t(x)$ that evolves from $\mathcal{N}(0, I_d)$ to $p_{data}$. With an optimal transport probability path that drives noise $\epsilon$ to $\textbf{z}$ with constant direction and speed in time~\cite{lipman2022flow}, the conditional flow matching (CFM) model $\textbf{v}_t^\theta(x)$ learns the conditional vector field $\textbf{v}^*_t(x|\mathbf{z})$ per sampled point $x$ from conditional vector field by minimizing the mean-squared loss. :
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}(\theta)&=
\mathbb{E}_{\substack{
t\sim \mathrm{Unif}[0,1],\\
\mathbf{z}\sim p_{\mathrm{data}},\\ \mathbf{x}\sim p_t(\cdot\mid z)
}} \notag
\!\Bigl[\bigl\|\textbf{v}_t^\theta(\mathbf{x})-\textbf{v}_t^*(\mathbf{x}\mid \mathbf{z})\bigr\|^2\Bigr]
\label{eq:cfm}\\
&=\mathbb{E}_{\substack{
t\sim \mathrm{Unif}[0,1],\\ \epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I_d),\\
\mathbf{z}\sim p_{\mathrm{data}}
}}
\!\Bigl[\bigl\|\textbf{v}_t^\theta(t\mathbf{z}+(1-t)\epsilon)-(\mathbf{z}-\epsilon)\bigr\|^2\Bigr].
\end{align}

To generate a policy using the CFM model, we first assume there is a ground-truth marginal vector field that transforms noise into an optimal policy given observations. In our case, we view the task of generating safe robot motion trajectories conditioned on local geometric observations and states $\mathbf{o}$, a safety parameter $\gamma$. In this case, we need to expand the expectation from \eqref{eq:cfm} over all possible $o$, $\gamma$, and $c$. Thus, we define our training objective as
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}(\theta)\!=\!
\mathbb{E}_{\substack{
t\sim \mathrm{Unif}[0,1],\\ \epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I_d),\\
(z,o,\gamma)\sim p_{\mathrm{data}}(z,o,\gamma)
}}
\!\!\!\!\Bigl[\bigl\|\textbf{v}_t^\theta(tz+(1-t)\epsilon\!\mid\! o,\gamma, c)-(z-\epsilon)\bigr\|^2\Bigr].
\label{eq:cfm_cost}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Discrete-Time Control Barrier Functions}
Let $\mathcal{C}=\{x\in\mathbb{R}^n:\; h(x)\ge 0\}$ denote the safe set for a continuously differentiable $h$.
For the discrete‐time control‐affine system
\begin{equation}
    x_{k+1}=f(x_k)+g(x_k)u_k, \label{eq:affinesystem}
\end{equation}
a function $h$ is a discrete‐time control barrier function (DCBF) associated with the control system \eqref{eq:affinesystem} if there exists $\gamma\in(0,1]$ such that for all $x_k$ there exists a control $u_k$ with
\begin{equation}
    \Delta h(x_k,u_k)\;:=\;h(x_{k+1})-h(x_k)\;\ge\;-\gamma\,h(x_k).
    \label{eq:dtcbf}
\end{equation}
Condition \eqref{eq:dtcbf} implies the one‐step decay bound
\begin{equation*}
    h(x_{k+1})\;\ge\;(1-\gamma)\,h(x_k)
    \, \Longrightarrow \,
    h(x_k)\;\ge\;(1-\gamma)^{k}\,h(x_0),
    \label{eq:exp_decay}
\end{equation*}
\textit{i.e.,} the lower bound on the barrier value decays exponentially with base $(1-\gamma)$, ensuring forward invariance of $\mathcal{C}$ \cite{ames2014cbf,agrawal2017discrete}.

\noindent \textbf{Remark.} In order to prove $h$ is a valid DCBF, it is necessary to find a point-wise set of control values at any given state is non-empty, with corresponding $\gamma$. To be compatible with the averaging in MPPI, we assumed that in case of at least one of the perturbed control \eqref{eq:mppi_rollout} respects \eqref{eq:dtcbf} at every state during the deployment of MPPI, we only used that successful actions as a data to train the generative policy.
\note{DCBF to BF, safe set to implicit safe set}
We define the safety specification that is derived from a supporting hyperplane to the single obstacle. Let \(\mathbf{q}_{k+i \mid i}\in\mathbb R^d\) denote the robot position at state \(\mathbf{x}_{k+i \mid i}\) at discrete time step $k$ and horizon $i = 0, ..., H-1$. For a convex obstacle, let $\mathbf{p}$ be the point on its boundary closest to the current position $\mathbf{q}_{k\mid k}$, with outward normal $\mathbf{n}$. We define the barrier function as
\begin{equation}
h^{\mathrm{aff}}(\mathbf{q};\,\mathbf{q}_{k\mid k}) :=
\frac{\mathbf{n}^\top(\mathbf{q}-\mathbf{p})}{\mathbf{n}^\top(\mathbf{q}_{k\mid k}-\mathbf{p})}.
\label{eq:aff_barrier}
\end{equation}
The implicit safe set for each horizon $i$ is defined as
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{C}_i^{\mathrm{aff}}=\{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_x} \mid h^{\mathrm{aff}}(\mathbf{q}_{k+i\mid k}; \mathbf{q}_{k\mid k})\ge 0\},\label{eq:aff_safeset}
\end{equation}
is a half-space, which is convex. Iterating the condition \eqref{eq:dtcbf} from $i=0$ with the normalization $h^{\mathrm{aff}}(\mathbf{q}_{k\mid k};\mathbf{q}_{k\mid k})=1$ yields an exponentially decaying lower bound on the safety specification along the prediction horizon:
\begin{equation}
h^{\mathrm{aff}}(\mathbf{q}_{k+i\mid k};\mathbf{q}_{k\mid k}) \ge (1-\gamma)^i. \quad i = 0, ..., H.
\label{eq:safetyspecification}
\end{equation}
% Applying the condition \eqref{eq:dtcbf} to this affine barrier function provides our safety specification for each rollout:
% \begin{equation}
% h^{\mathrm{aff}}(x_{k+i+1\mid k};x_{k\mid k}) \ge (1-\gamma) h^{\mathrm{aff}}(x_{k+i\mid k};x_{k\mid k}).
% \end{equation}
\noindent \textbf{Nominal Polytope for MPPI safety.} We further define the notion of a \textit{nominal polytope} derived from above safety specification to generalize safety definition for multiple obstacles. 
We assume that the robot can only sense obstacles within a finite radial distance $R$, and that the index of obstacles that intersect within that region at the current position is denoted by $\mathcal{O}(q_{k \mid k})$. The barrier function is defined as the minimum of the affine barrier functions \eqref{eq:safetyspecification} over obstacles in the sensing region:
\begin{equation}
    h^{\text{poly}}(\mathbf{q};\mathbf{q}_{k\mid k}) = \min_{j\in\mathcal{O}_j} h^{\mathrm{aff}}(\mathbf{q};\mathbf{q}_{k\mid k})
\end{equation}
The implicit safe set for each horizon $i=0,\cdots,H-1$,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{C}^{\text{poly}}_i=\{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_x} \mid h^{\text{poly}}(\mathbf{q}_{k+i\mid k}; \mathbf{q}_{k\mid k})\ge (1-\gamma)^i\},\label{eq:poly_safeset}
\end{equation}

This ego-centric barrier function ensures that the robot maintains a safe distance from all obstacles throughout the prediction horizon. For example, the top of Fig. \ref{fig:polytope} illustrates safe sets \eqref{eq:aff_safeset}.

\section{Problem Definition}

To achieve safety-critical generation, we first formulate our safe planning objective as a constrained optimization problem. We introduce a nominal polytope constraint into the standard MPPI formulation \eqref{eq:sc-mppi} to ensure forward invariance of safe sets. Our constrained MPPI problem is defined as:
\begin{subequations}
\label{eq:sc-mppi}
\begin{align*}
\min_{\{\mathbf{u}_{k:k+H-1\mid k}\}}
& \mathbb{E}\!\left[J(\mathbf{x}_{k}, \mathbf{u}_{k+i\mid k})
\right], \tag{1a} \label{eq:mppi-dcbf}\\
\text{s.t.}\quad
& \eqref{eq:mppi-dyn}, \ \eqref{eq:mppi-set1}, \ \eqref{eq:mppi-set2},\\
& h^{\text{poly}}(\mathbf{q}_{k+i\mid k}; \mathbf{q}_{k\mid k})\ge (1-\gamma)^i. \tag{1e} \label{eq:poly}
\end{align*}
\end{subequations}

Here, \eqref{eq:poly} enforces a discrete-time control barrier constraint over the prediction in the original MPPI formulation. While direct optimization of this non-convex, constrained problem is typically intractable in cluttered environments, our algorithm efficiently solves it via parallel sampling.

\noindent \textbf{Remark 1.} 
Direct optimization using non-convex control barrier function that replaces $h^\text{poly}$ reduces to the problem MPC-CBF in the previous work \cite{zeng2021safety}. Despite the recursive feasibility of both problems not being generally guaranteed, from the view of set invariance, the shared safety parameter, $\gamma$, modulates the trade-off between safety and local feasibility. 
% (Here say something about their optimization problem's downside e.g., sensitive to initialization and struggle in dynamic settings...) 
However, the non-convex nature of the resulting optimization problem on arbitrary $J(\mathbf{x}_{k}, \mathbf{u}_{k+i\mid k})$ often makes real-time implementation computationally intractable \cite{rawlings2020model}. SBPC methods such as MPPI face similar challenges, and often mitigate pointwise infeasibility by shaping the sampling distribution \cite{yin2022, yin2023shield}, but this harms the optimality condition using importance sampling (???). 

\noindent \textbf{Remark 2.}
A valid control barrier function on $\mathcal{U}=\mathbb{R}^{d_u}$ is usually assumed in prior works\footnote{If we assume $\mathcal{X}$ as a controlled invariant set, then the reachable set is also guaranteed to be non-empty.}. However, one must verify that there exists a \textit{feasible input-constrained controller} (\textit{i.e.,} input bounds in $\mathcal{U}\neq\mathbb{R}^{d_u}$), and promising approaches to address this are backup control barrier functions (bCBFs) \cite{gurriet2018online, van2026generalizations}. However in practice, constructing bCBF in a task-agnostic way is challenging. 


For these reasons, our planner relies on the weaker but more practical condition of pointwise feasibility without harming optimality condition. Rather than proving recursive feasibility, our approach ensures safety by enforcing DCBF constraints at each time step. This also motivates our next goal to develop safe flow expansion mechanism, where the data is discontinued to planner's infeasibility (\textit{i.e., OOD distribution}). We rather self-generate the safe behavior and enforce more relaxed DCBF constraints. Rather than relying purely on online computation, we view the proposed safe flow expansion as a mechanism for \textit{distilling} this safe behavior into generative policy. Having established the problem statement, we direct our attention to an example to answer the question: \textit{How can we train a generative model that also exhibits this safe behavior in out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios?}

Our proposed algorithm, model predictive path integral control with DCBF-based sample rejection (MPPI-DCBF), modifies the standard MPPI framework to formally enforce safety. The core of our method is the safety specification in \eqref{eq:poly}, which is designed to guarantee the forward invariance of the sequence of time-varying safe sets $\mathcal{P}_i$ defined in \eqref{eq:poly_safeset}. This is achieved by integrating the DCBF condition directly into the sampling step of the MPPI algorithm. Any sampled rollout that violates the condition is rejected; consequently, the optimal control sequence \eqref{eq:mppi-update} is computed as a convex combination of only those rollouts that are certified to be safe. Under the assumption of pointwise feasibility\footnote{For the discrete-time control affine system \eqref{eq:affinesystem} and for any initial state $x_{k|k} \in \mathcal{C}_i^\mathrm{aff}$, there exists at least one control sequence $\{u_{k+i|k}\}_{i=0}^{N-1}$ such that the resulting trajectory $\{x_{k+i|k}\}_{i=1}^{N}$ lies within the sequence of safe sets defined in \eqref{eq:aff_safeset}, i.e., $x_{k+i|k} \in \mathcal{C}_i^{\mathrm{aff}}$ for all $i \in \{1, \dots, N\}$.

\begin{proposition} \label{prop: 1}
    For the discrete-time control affine system \eqref{eq:affinesystem}, if all contributing rollouts $(m)$ satisfy $\mathbf{x}^{(m)}_{k+1\mid k} \in \mathcal{C}_1^{\mathrm{poly}}$, then the averaged state $\mathbf{x}^*_{k+1|k}$ under the MPPI-DCBF control is also guaranteed to be in the safe set, \textit{i.e.,} $\mathbf{x}^*_{k+1|k} \in \mathcal{C}_1^{\mathrm{poly}}$.
    
    \begin{proof}
    Under the control affine dynamics, the state $\mathbf{x}^{(m)}_{k+1\mid k}$ in the next time step is an affine function of the control inputs. Since the admissible inputs are a convex polytope and the control update of MPPI is a convex combination of control perturbations~\eqref{eq:mppi-update}, the resulting state $\mathbf{x}^*_{k+1\mid k}$ is a convex combination of the rollout states:
    \begin{align}
    \mathbf{x}^*_{k+1|k} = \sum_m \hat{\omega}_m \mathbf{x}^{(m)}_{k+1|k}, \nonumber
    \end{align}
    where $\hat{\omega}_m \in \mathbb{R}_{> 0}$ and $\sum_m \hat{\omega}_m = 1$ are the importance weights derived from \eqref{eq:mppi-weights} after the rejection of unsafe samples. Moreover, since the safe set $\mathcal{C}_1^{\mathrm{aff}}$ is a convex half-space, any convex combination of points within it must also lie within the set. Thus, if $\mathbf{x}^{(m)}_{k+1\mid k} \in \mathcal{C}_1^{\mathrm{poly}}$ for all $m$, it follows that $\mathbf{x}^*_{k+1|k} \in \mathcal{C}_1^{\mathrm{aff}}$.
    \end{proof}
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Verifying predicted action via verifier polytope}
Before we introduce our main algorithm, here we define the deterministic verifier function that ensures the trajectory respects multi-step safety. We named this polytope as \textit{verifier polytope} and separates the term with the nominal polytope \eqref{eq:poly}, and it is is constructed along the prediction horizon $H$ in its trajectory yield by the generated action. The optimization problem is solved for each nearby obstacle within the sensing region. 

\noindent \textbf{Verifier Polytope for Expansion.} Concretely, let \(c=q_0\in\mathbb R^d\) denote the current robot position in the free space \(\mathcal{Q}\) and let
\(\{q_t\}_{t=0}^{H}\) be a candidate local trajectory submitted to verifier, with
\(p_t:=q_t-c\).  For a barrier parameter \(\gamma\in(0,1]\), define
\(\alpha_t=(1-\gamma)^t\) and \(\beta_t=1-\alpha_t\). We then formulate under each sensed circular obstacle that is represented as \(\mathcal O_i=\{x\in\mathbb R^d:\|x-o_i\|_2\le r_i\}\) where \(o_i\) and \(r_i\) are its center and radius. Instead of fixing the obstacle face normal, we seek to find a variable face \(\mathbf{a}_i^\top(x-c)\le m_i,\), that respects receding horizon safety \eqref{}, where \(a_i\in\mathbb R^d\) is the verifier face normal and \(m_i\in\mathbb R\)
is its robot-centered margin. This can be formulated as a second-order-cone-program (SOCP) for each obstacle boundary:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\max_{\mathbf{a}_i}\ & m_i\\
\text{s.t. }\ & \mathbf{a}_i^\top(\mathbf{q}_t-c)\le\big(1-(1-\gamma)^t\big)m_i \quad (t=1,\dots,H),\\
& r_i\|\mathbf{a}_i\|_2\le \mathbf{a}_i^\top(o_i-c)-m_i,\\
&\|\mathbf{a}_i\|_2\le1,\ m_i\ge m_{\min}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:socp}
\end{equation}

The first constraint is the rollout level-set condition
\(H_i(q_t)\ge\alpha_t\), since
\(H_i(q_t)=(m_i-a_i^\top p_t)/m_i\).  The second constraint separates the
entire disk, because
\(\min_{x\in\mathcal O_i}a_i^\top(x-c)
=a_i^\top(o_i-c)-r_i\|a_i\|_2\).  The norm bound
\(\|a_i\|_2\le1\) is the unit-normal constraint, and \(m_i\ge m_{\min}\) prevents degenerate faces. After solving all per-obstacle
faces, the verifier polytope has the form \footnote{In order to guarantee the bounded verifier polytope in the sensing region, we solve \eqref{eq:socp} for auxiliary obstacles that is tangent to the largest K-sided polytope; K = 16 is used.}
\[
\mathcal P
=
\bigcap_i
\{x:a_i^\top(x-c)\le m_i\},
\]
% or equivalently \(Ax\le b\) with \(A_i=a_i^\top\) and
% \(b_i=a_i^\top c+m_i\). 
In this way, the validity of sampled control policies is evaluated by the existence of the verifier polytope in terms of multi-step safety, \textit{i.e., $\tilde v(\mathbf{\hat u}) =1$}.

\begin{proposition} \label{prop: 2}A stochastic policy $\mathbf{u}_{k:k+H-1\mid k}$ with length $H$ produced by MPPI-DCBF~\eqref{eq:mppi-dcbf}, which is the constrained stochastic optimization problem solution under the outward normal $n$ from \eqref{eq:aff_barrier}, is a valid multi-step samples:
\begin{equation*}
\tilde v(\mathbf{u}_{k:k+H-1\mid k)} =1
\end{equation*}
\end{proposition}
\subsection{Safe Flow Expansion}
Generative policies constrain actions to the distribution of their demonstrations. Their behavior is usually limited to the dominant modes present in the demonstration data. This becomes problematic in OOD scenarios, where the model encounters states unseen during training. To address this distribution shift, recent large-scale imitation learning frameworks attempt to gather massive amounts of human intervention data to fill the coverage gaps \cite{}. However, this brute-force data-collection paradigm is inherently unscalable for capturing all possible safety-critical actions. To this end, we can utilize the self-generation of the generative policies that can query safe actions and one can \textit{expand} the region likely to be sampled from a pre-trained generative model~ \cite{de2026active}. 

Concretely, for a given context $(\mathbf o,\gamma)$, the pre-trained policy $p_1^{\theta_{\mathrm{0}}}(\cdot\mid\mathbf
o,\gamma)$  samples only its \emph{generable set}:
\begin{equation}
\Omega^{\tau}_{\theta_{\mathrm{pre}}}
(\mathbf o,\gamma)=\{\hat{\mathbf u}: p_1^{\theta_{\mathrm{pre}}}(\hat{\mathbf u}\mid\mathbf
o,\gamma)\ge\tau\},
\end{equation}
which is the region likely to be sampled and preferably draws from dominant modes. Here, we abbreviate and refer to the sample as $H$ steps of predicted actions $\mathbf{\hat u}_{k:k+H-1}$ as $\mathbf{\hat u}$. 

Let $\Omega^{*}(\mathbf o,\gamma)=\{\hat{\mathbf u}: v(\hat{\mathbf u};\mathbf o,\gamma)=1\}$
denote the \emph{valid action set} certified by the verifier polytope of~\eqref{eq:socp}. Since the planner explores few dominant modes due to \textit{local infeasibility}, $\Omega^{\tau}_
{\theta_{\mathrm{pre}}}\subset\Omega^{*}$ with $\mathrm{Vol}(\Omega^{\tau}_{\theta_{\mathrm{pre}}})
\ll\mathrm{Vol}(\Omega^{*})$. Valid samples will lie in the valid out-of-distribution region $\Omega^{*}\setminus\Omega^{\tau}_
{\theta_{\mathrm{pre}}}$. 

% Here we introduce our flow expansion mechanism, contingent to \cite{de2026active}. For the sake of simplicity, we refer to the sample as $H$ steps of predicted actions ($\mathbf{\hat u}_{k:k+H-1}$). We self-generate the action prediction at each time step and execute predicted actions. This yields trajectory generation, and for those that complete the task (\textit{i.e.,} reach the goal while maintained safe) each action prediction assigned a binary function value of $v(\mathbf{\hat u}_{k:k+H-1})$ viz \eqref{eq:verifier}. 

To self-generate the sample to be informative about the verifier polytope while staying near the current policy ${\theta_t}$, at the round $t$: 
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{\hat u}\sim\ p_1^{\theta_t}(\mathbf{\hat u})\,\exp\!\Big(\sigma_t\big(\phi_s(\mathbf{\hat u})\big)/\beta\Big)/Z,
\label{eq:importancesampling}
\end{equation}
where $Z$ is the normalization $\int p(\mathbf{\hat u})\exp\!\big(f(\mathbf{\hat u})/\beta\big)\,dx$, with $f(\mathbf{\hat u}):=\sigma_t(\phi_s(\mathbf{\hat u}))$, $p:=p_1^{\theta_t}$. Notably, $\phi_s(\mathbf{\hat u})$ is a noised flow representation, which is modeled as the penultimate hidden activation (the feature before the head of the target conditional flow matching vector field).

\section{Conclusion}
This paper presented a framework for creating safety-tunable generative control policies for safety-critical tasks. We introduced a novel MPPI-DCBF planner that uses DCBFs to generate a rich dataset of provably safe multi-modal trajectories for linear systems. By training a conditional flow matching model on this data, we produced a real-time policy that can modulate its safety and feasibility via a single parameter at inference time, without the need for online sampling. Our experiments on 2D and 3D systems validate the approach, demonstrating a practical method for deploying safe, adaptable, and high-performance learned controllers.

A limitation of our current generative model is that it inherits safety statistically from the data and lacks its own formal certificate. Future work could explore combining our generative policy with a lightweight run-time safety filter to provide hard guarantees. Additionally, learning an optimal $\gamma$ schedule directly from data remains an interesting open problem.
